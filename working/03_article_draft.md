Another year done, another stack of monthly energy data to dig through. This is the fifth year running that I've written up the performance of our home photovoltaic system, and at this point the data starts to tell a real story — one of steady production, rising consumption, and the ever-growing importance of every kilowatt-hour we generate ourselves.

If you're researching home solar in Germany — whether you're deciding whether to install a system, wondering what realistic numbers look like after a few years, or trying to benchmark your own system — hopefully this post gives you something useful. Our setup: a <!-- TODO: confirm kWp --> system on the roof of our house in southern Germany, installed in 2021, with a BYD battery for storage and a SolarEdge inverter. We also have a Keba P30 EV charger and, since mid-2025, a Polestar 4 that we try to charge primarily on solar surplus via [evcc](/intro-into-evcc-charging/).

## 2025: Steady Production, Higher Consumption

In 2025, our panels produced a total of **8,624 kWh** — down just 0.9% from the 8,706 kWh we recorded in 2024. Essentially flat. Our total household consumption, on the other hand, climbed to **10,714 kWh**, a 6.3% increase over 2024. The new EV is almost certainly responsible for most of that jump.

The seasonal pattern looked familiar:

- **Best month: June**, with 1,291 kWh — narrowly ahead of May (1,260 kWh). For the first time in five years, June rather than July took the top spot.
- **Worst month: January**, as always — just 169 kWh in a month where we consumed over 1,000 kWh.
- **Most grid-independent months:** July and June, where we pulled only 104 kWh and 161 kWh respectively from the grid.

What changed compared to 2024? The spring was noticeably stronger. February was +55 kWh, May was +99 kWh. But July let us down — 1,074 kWh against July 2024's 1,299 kWh, a drop of 225 kWh. Whatever was happening weather-wise that month, it hit us hard. October was similarly disappointing (352 kWh vs. 482 kWh in 2024).

The flip side: December 2025 was almost exactly double December 2024 — 166 kWh versus 85 kWh. That fits a pattern we've noticed over the last few years: winters are getting slightly milder, with less snow cover on the panels. It's a small number in absolute terms, but it's consistent.

A few things jump out. 2022 was our best production year by some margin (9,320 kWh) — we had an exceptionally sunny spring that year and it's never been repeated. Since then, we've settled into a band of 8,400–8,700 kWh, which is probably where we'll stay barring something unusual.

The self-consumed solar column is the one I watch most closely. It's gone from 5,044 kWh in year one to 5,761 kWh in year five — a 14% increase, even though production has barely moved. The battery is doing its job. We now consistently use about two-thirds of what we produce on-site, rather than exporting it for a few cents per kWh.

## The Financial Reality

This is the part people usually want to know. What does it actually mean for the electricity bill?

In 2025, we self-consumed **5,761 kWh** of solar energy. German residential electricity prices averaged around **€0.38/kWh** in 2025 — still the highest in the EU, despite a slight decline from the 2023 peak of €0.47/kWh. At that rate, those 5,761 kWh represent roughly **€2,189 in avoided grid costs**.

We also exported 2,833 kWh back to the grid. The current feed-in tariff for a small rooftop system like ours is around **7.9 ct/kWh** — which adds up to about **€224** in feed-in revenue.

Combined, that's roughly **€2,400 of financial benefit in 2025 alone**.

Over five years, we've self-consumed an estimated 27,400 kWh in total. Even at a conservative blended average of €0.30/kWh (accounting for lower prices in earlier years), that's around **€8,200 in cumulative grid savings** — plus another ~€1,000 or so in feed-in revenue over the same period.

## How Do We Compare to Other German Systems?

For southern Germany, the typical yield is **1,000–1,200 kWh per kWp of installed capacity per year**. A well-optimized system in Bavaria can reach the higher end of that range. Our production of 8,624 kWh is consistent with a <!-- TODO: kWp --> system operating in a typical southern Germany year.

Nationally, 2025 was an exceptionally sunny year — Germany recorded around 1,945 sunshine hours, roughly 26% above the long-term mean. Total PV output across Germany jumped 21%. Our flat production compared to 2024 suggests that our local weather — particularly that weak July — diverged from the national average. We weren't the only ones: it seems a few regions had a cloudier-than-average midsummer even in what was otherwise a sunny year.

Our self-consumption rate of **66.8%** (the proportion of our production that we use on-site rather than export) compares well against German benchmarks. For systems without battery storage, typical self-consumption rates are 25–35% of production. With a battery, 60–80% is achievable. We're in the top part of that range, which validates the decision to pair the PV system with storage from the start.

## What's Next

We have a smart meter now — it was installed by our grid provider in late 2025. The obvious next step is switching to a dynamic electricity tariff. From January 2025, all German providers are legally required to offer dynamic tariffs, and players like Tibber and aWATTar make it increasingly accessible. On days with high solar generation and low grid demand, wholesale prices can actually go negative — meaning you'd be paid to consume electricity. That happened in Germany for the first time in May 2025.

Combined with evcc and the Polestar battery, a dynamic tariff could let us time grid consumption for cheap or free periods while the solar system covers the daytime baseload. The pieces are in place; we just need to make the switch.

Beyond that? We're not planning any changes to the PV system itself. After five years, the panels are performing as expected with no obvious degradation. The BYD battery is healthy. The SolarEdge inverter quietly does its thing. Sometimes the most interesting thing to report is that everything just... works.

One question I do want to explore: now that we have an EV, does the math shift enough to justify expanding the system? Our consumption has crossed 10,700 kWh, and if we add more EV miles, it could climb further. At some point, additional panels might make more sense than continuing to buy an extra 4,900 kWh from the grid each year.
